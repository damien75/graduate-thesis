\SetAbstractEnName{\MakeUppercase{Abstract}}%
\SetAbstractEnText{ This thesis uses a frame fitting method developed in \cite{piuzephd} to describe the fiber geometry of mammal hearts. Extending previous work, we analyze on a quantitative and qualitative approach the extra knowledge that this geometrical analysis can give us on infarcted hearts. Those hearts, right after the infarct, see the infarct region get filled with collagen and significantly lose contractile efficiency as many fibers that were involved in the contraction during the beat cycle died during the infarct. General knowledge from ADC and FA map analysis lack in the field of showing how some surviving fibers can persist in the infarct region and how the reconstruction process can lead to a persistence of the contractile function although it is greatly hindered and modified by the geometrical reorganization and the decreased connectivity between regions remote from the infarct and potential surviving fibers in the infarct region. Our connection form parameter calculation and frame fitting error plots allow us to have an interesting visualization of regions mostly filled with collagen within the infarct region, as well as regions where very interestingly some fiber structure still shows up and demonstrate contractile potential after infarct, and how this new organization appears to be. We conclude that this approach could be a very promising way to understand the process of reconstruction through which the heart goes after infarct. This could also bring out the new geometrical structure and its relation to the unfortunately frequent secondary effect: tachycardia, arrhythmia and else. We suggest the possibility to perform more analysis of these kinds and especially on more time frames as our analysis is only focused on pig hearts 6 weeks after infarcts, as we did not have cases that followed the evolution before this 6 weeks mark and later on.}
\AbstractEn%
